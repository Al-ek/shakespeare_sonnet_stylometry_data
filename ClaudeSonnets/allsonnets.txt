I

When time doth lay his finger on the brow
Of she whose beauty summer hath confessed,
Shall I not cry against his cruel vow
That robs the fairest rose of all the rest?
For in her eyes the morning finds its glass,
And every dawn doth borrow light from her,
Yet all fair things must wither as the grass
When autumn's cold doth through the meadows stir.
But verse shall be the vault where beauty sleeps,
Impervious to the frost that kills the flower,
And what the grave in darkness blindly keeps,
My lines shall give again in deathless hour.
  So long as men draw breath and eyes can see,
  Thy face shall live immortal here in me.

II

Thou art the season that I cannot name,
Not spring nor autumn, neither cold nor warm,
But something past the reach of nature's frame,
A stillness at the center of the storm.
When I am far from thee the hours grow vast
And every minute doubles in its weight,
As ships becalmed upon a windless mast
Must hang between their longing and their fate.
But when thy presence fills the narrow room
The very walls lean inward, glad to hear
Thy voice that drives the winter from its gloom
And makes of every month the turning year.
  What art thou then, that season without name?
  Thou art my world—and I thy foolish flame.

III

Look in the mirror and confess to thee
What thou wouldst hide from all the watching world:
That beauty is a debt, and being free
Thou hast not paid it back with love unfurled.
Thy mother once was young as thou art now,
And in her face thy face was first designed,
She gave thee all: the cheekbone and the brow,
The gentle wit, the quick and curious mind.
So shalt thou give, or else those gifts must die
When thou art gone and none shall take thine art,
No child shall look upon the winter sky
With eyes that hold the warmth of thine own heart.
  The world is but a glass that shows the past;
  Give back thy gifts, that they may ever last.

IV

How often have I cursed the fading light
That steals the hours before my work is done,
And railed against the early closing night
That cuts me off from thee before begun?
For day is but the promise of thy face,
And all the business of the waking hours
Is but a long and stumbling, graceless race
Toward thee, the end of all my hopeful powers.
Yet night itself, when thou art lying near,
Becomes a kingdom richer than the day,
And darkness is no darkness when I hear
Thy breathing soft upon the pillow's grey.
  So cursed is light that keeps me from thy side,
  And blessed dark in which my heart doth bide.

V

What summer hoards, the jealous frost shall claim,
And all the gold that filled the meadow's cup
Shall answer to December's bitter aim
When harvest hath been drunk and given up.
For time doth keep no covenant with fair,
Nor seal his mercy on the blooming cheek,
But strips the bough of all it once did wear
And leaves the shivering branch both pale and weak.
Yet beauty hath a remedy in kind:
If she that owns it lend it to a child,
Then what the hand of winter leaves behind
Shall bloom again in features undefiled.
  So yield thy gifts to love before they pass,
  And see thyself renewed in beauty's glass.

VI

Shall I compare thee to a winter fire
That warms the room but cannot warm the soul?
Or art thou rather some unnamed desire
That makes of every half a trembling whole?
I have seen beauty in the open field
When bluebells nod in unison with wind,
And watched how willows to the river yield
As lovers yield to what they most have sinned.
But none of these hath held me like thy gaze
That falls on me and fixes me in place,
As amber holds the creature of past days
Suspended in the amber of thy grace.
  Compare thee? No. All comparison must fail.
  Thou art the truth whereof all else is tale.

VII

When morning lifts her lantern o'er the hill
And gilds the world with promises of gold,
The creatures of the field stand hushed and still
To watch that ancient pageantry unfold.
So thou, when first thou enter'st any room,
Dost draw all eyes as candles draw the moth,
And faces lift from shadow and from gloom
As fields receive the sun-drenched morning cloth.
But morning, having climbed to her high place,
Doth find by noon the world hath turned away,
And all who praised the glory of her face
Find new devotions as she dips toward grey.
  Then use thy morning well before the fall,
  For borrowed light at last returneth all.

VIII

Why doth sweet sound make sorrowful thine ear
When harmony should set the spirit free?
The lute that weeps hath still a note most clear,
And concord sings what silence fails to see.
Consider how the choir of many parts
Doth bind its separate voices into one,
As scattered embers, drawn to kindred hearts,
Compose a single flame against the sun.
The instrument that hangs unplucked on high
Doth waste its music in the empty air,
And all its latent song must fade and die
Unless some willing hand doth tune it there.
  So thou, who hast a song yet left unsung,
  Must find thy consort now, while yet thou'rt young.

IX

How careful was I when I took in hand
The tender project of thy early love,
As one who builds upon uncertain sand
And prays for mercy from the skies above.
For love first spoken is a fragile thing,
Like moth-wing patterns pressed upon the air,
A breath may break it, and the words we bring
May serve to wound the very heart laid bare.
Yet I was bold, and boldness found its grace,
For thou didst meet my faltering words with thine,
And in the meeting of our gaze and face
I drank thee off like consecrated wine.
  So blessed is boldness when the heart is true,
  And twice so blessed when it is met by you.

X

Wilt thou deny that thou hast love to spare
For one beyond thyself, yet turn aside?
To hoard one's heart is a most grievous snare,
And loveless thrift but swells the self with pride.
Consider: when the miser grips his gold
And will not spend it where the needy stand,
The coin grows cold, the story is soon told,
And wealth decays in the ungiving hand.
So beauty kept from love's appointed use
Doth rot the very bloom it means to save,
And she who will not let affection loose
Doth dig for love a solitary grave.
  Then be not cruel in thy beauty's reign;
  What thou withholdst in joy returns in pain.

XI

O that thou wert as thou once wert to me,
Before the world had written on thy face
Those lines of care that now I barely see
Through the fond habit of familiar grace.
I do not mourn the lines—they are thine own,
And every crease of laughter round thine eyes
Is dearer to me than the seed first sown
Of beauty fresh beneath unopened skies.
But I do mourn the distance time hath made
Between the you who loved me without thought
And this more careful you, who art afraid
To give what once so freely thou hadst brought.
  Come back to me across that narrow space,
  And let us be again what time would erase.

XII

How shall I mark the turning of the year
When every season steals some cherished thing?
The violet bows its head upon the bier
And yields the field to autumn's withering.
I see the oak grow gaunt against the sky
Where once it wore its canopy of green,
And all the bright-eyed summer days gone by
Lie pressed like flowers where summer once had been.
When I behold thee in thy flourishing
I think of harvests gathered, then forgot,
Of how the thrush grows quiet after spring
And sings no more when summer finds her not.
  Yet I shall hold thee past the season's art:
  Time takes the face, but not what's in the heart.

XIII

The absent heart is not the faithless one,
Though distance lends itself to doubt's designs,
As shadows multiply when sets the sun
And one frail tree becomes a thousand pines.
I have been gone from thee these many weeks
And all the while thy face before me burned,
A lantern that the homesick traveler seeks
When he hath strayed and longs to be returned.
Doubt thou the stars are fire if thou must,
Doubt that the sun doth move, doubt truth a lie,
But never doubt that in my every gust
Of longing breath thy name is my first cry.
  The farthest distance only serves to prove
  How close thou art in the geography of love.

XIV

My knowledge comes not from the turning spheres,
Nor do I read my fortune in the sky,
No augur's art hath schooled these later years
To tell me whether good or ill draws nigh.
The comet's blaze reveals no truth to me,
Nor can I parse what planetary sign
Doth govern what shall come or cease to be,
Or bend the will of mortal to divine.
Yet in thy countenance I read most clear
What no astronomer hath reckoned yet:
That where thou art, there beauty need not fear,
And truth hath found a face it shan't forget.
  But if thou turn from both, my stars go dark,
  And beauty's age is ended with thy spark.

XV

A different autumn settles now in me,
Not that loud season gilt with harvest gold,
But something quieter, a falling free
Of all the urgencies that made me bold.
I see it in the mirror's morning face,
Where time hath penned his marginal critique,
And left his silver notings in the place
Where once my darker certainties would speak.
Yet do not pity what thou seest there,
For autumn hath its dignities and ease,
A slower warmth, a more considered care,
A sweeter fruit that hangs on yellowed trees.
  So love me in this season of the year:
  What summer promised, autumn shall make clear.

XVI

What stronger weapon hast thou than this verse
To hold thy beauty from the tyrant's claim?
Though I may carve thee into every terse
And labored line, the carving is not fame.
The fairest portrait hung in gallery halls
Doth only hold the surface of the eye,
And painted beauty on the painter's walls
Doth smile the same as summer hurtles by.
But thou couldst give thyself in living kind
And leave behind a warmer, truer art,
Some child whose eye doth carry forth thy mind,
And beat thy rhythm in a younger heart.
  No pen of mine can do what nature can:
  Live on through love, and not through any man.

XVII

What future reader, turning these thin leaves,
Shall credit what I set so plainly here?
That beauty such as thine the eye conceives
But once, if fortune lend the lucky year?
They'll say I dealt in a fantastical trade,
That I exaggerated every grace,
That no such living creature could have made
So wild an argument of form and face.
And I shall not be there to swear it true,
Nor bring them to the light where thou dost stand,
And bid them look an hour or two on you
And tell me then my praise was merely planned.
  So let some child of thine outlive this page,
  And prove my foolish verse was not old age.

XVIII

The reckless youth who squanders all his light
On pleasures brief and merrymaking vain
Shall wake one morning to a greyer sight
And count the cost of all that careless gain.
Not so with thee, who hast been careful, wise,
Who hast set love aside like winter stores
Against the season when the cold wind cries
And frost comes begging at the bolted doors.
But care too great is cousin to despair,
And prudence kept too long becomes a chain,
The miser guards his gold with equal prayer
As he who throws it to the summer rain.
  So be not over-thrifty with thy heart:
  Spend freely now, before the long depart.

XIX

Thou, time, hast felled the cedar and the oak,
And turned the river from its ancient bed,
And wrapped the waking world in winter's cloak
When all the flowers wished for warmth instead.
Thou hast unmade the faces of the great
And worn the lettered stone to nameless smooth,
And made of every kingdom desolate
What once no siege of war could dare to move.
Go then, do all the damage that thou wilt
To hill and valley, season, stone, and tide,
But leave untouched the beauty thou hast built
In her for whom these trembling lines are tried.
  Do what thou wilt to all the world beside,
  But lay no ruin where my love doth bide.

XX

So now I lay this little book of hours
Before thee like a garland, roughly made
From such poor blossoms as my garden flowers,
More weed than rose, more stubborn than afraid.
If any line hath caught the light aright,
If any phrase hath held what I have meant,
Then I am grateful for that borrowed light
And count my crooked effort well-spent.
For thou hast been my subject and my cause,
My north star and my wandering southern wind,
The text upon which every poem draws
Its meaning, like a lamp that lights the blind.
  Forgive what falls short of what thou art:
  No words were ever equal to this heart.
