CCXV

When winter's breath hath stripped the elm tree bare,
And frost doth seal the murmuring brook in glass,
What comfort then remains for those who wear
The heavy cloak of time that none may pass?
Yet in the marrow of the coldest night
Some ember keeps its counsel, red and low,
As love, once kindled, holds its threadbare light
Against the dark that all things come to know.
So thou, whose warmth did furnish all my days,
Art not diminished though the season turn,
For in the record of my grateful phrase
Thy name shall find a hearth wherein to burn:
    The frost may take the flower from the bough,
    But cannot frost the verse that keeps thee now.

CCXVI

What tongue hath ever shaped the proper word
For that which steals upon the sleeping heart,
As dawn arrives unbidden and unstirred,
And lights the chamber ere the sleeper start?
So came thy face upon my waking mind
Before I knew the name of what I saw,
A visitation of the sweetest kind
That left my former reason full of awe.
For love doth not announce itself with drums
Nor send before it any herald bright,
But like the tide without a whisper comes
And finds the shore already in its might.
    So I was taken ere I could defend,
    And gladly lost, and glad to stay thus end.

CCXVII

The sundial marks the hours that it may,
But clouds may come and steal the gnomon's art,
And so the measure of my longest day
Is reckoned not by sun but by thy heart.
When thou art present, time doth hold its tongue
And stands apart as one who dares not speak,
But absence is a bell forever rung
That fills each hour and hollows every week.
O that the dial of the world could freeze
Upon one golden afternoon in June,
When thou didst laugh and put my mind at ease
And summer hung contented with the moon:
    But clocks are blind to what a lover knows,
    And time moves on wherever sweetness goes.

CCXVIII

Shall I compare the tenure of thy grace
To monuments that proud antiquity
Hath raised in stone to fix some sovereign face
Against the long assault of memory?
The stone grows porous where the acid rains
Descend through centuries of patient toil,
And marble yields at last to what remains
When all the sculptor's confidence doth spoil.
But thou art not of stone, nor wouldst thou wish
The cold immortal prison that it gives,
For living breath is sweeter than this dish
Of deathless dust that neither breathes nor lives.
    Let stone praise stone — I'll have thee warm and brief,
    And mourn thy passing with a mortal grief.

CCXIX

My glass hath made me privy to the truth
That years do levy on a willing face,
How time doth slowly audit all the youth
And leaves behind a more considerate grace.
Yet what the mirror shows I do not fear,
For thou hast taught me that the body's sum
Is less than what the spirit holds most dear,
The depth below the skin that lines become.
Then let the temples silver, and the brow
Record in careful script the years it bore,
I would not barter what I carry now
For all the untried smoothness gone before:
    For I have loved and been by loving schooled,
    And by that learning, wisely been refueled.

CCXX

There is a tide in the affairs of spring
That crests upon the first warm breath of May,
When every common and ungainly thing
Puts on a borrowed beauty for the day.
The weed itself doth catch some golden light
And wear it like a circlet on its head,
And even stones seem softened and less bright
Against the green that wakes above the dead.
How much the more then doth thy natural grace
Exceed the borrowed finery of the year,
Who need'st no seasonal light upon thy face
To make the dullest hour grow sharp and clear:
    May lends its lustre to the meanest thing,
    But thou alone art cause enough for spring.

CCXXI

O thou that art the keeper of my days,
In whom my scattered purposes are stored,
Who turn'st my crooked wandering into ways
And mak'st my silence speak as with a chord:
What was I ere thy company was mine?
A vessel without pilot on the sea,
A lamp that held but would not give its shine,
A word unsaid that no one wished to free.
Now every morning wears thy name as light,
And every dusk is coloured with thy thought,
And all the middle hours that fill the night
Are threaded through with all that thou hast taught:
    I was a text without a margin's gloss,
    And thou the hand that made me speak my loss.

CCXXII

As rivers that have long been held in drought
Do rush with double fury when the rain
At last descends and breaks the dry spell out,
And flood the cracked and thirsty plain again:
So I, long parched by sorrow's governance,
Did meet thy kindness like a sudden flood,
And felt the whole arrested life advance
And quicken through the channels of my blood.
Too long the fields of me had known the heat
Of longing unassuaged by any hand,
Too long had I made famine into meat
And learnt to count as full what was but sand.
    Then came thy rain, and all the plains of me
    Grew green with more than I had hoped to see.

CCXXIII

What profit hath the astronomer of stars,
Who maps the patient courses of the sky
And reckons up the influences of Mars
Yet cannot say for whom those planets fly?
His tables tell him where the wanderers go
And what conjunctions govern what affairs,
But love observes no table that we know
And pays no heed to how the heavens square.
I would not have the stars dictate my heart
Nor place my choosing in the zodiac's hand,
For love is not so mathematical an art
As charts and spheres could ever understand:
    The stars may tell thee what the seasons bring,
    But only thou canst tell me everything.

CCXXIV

How many poets, grey with borrowed praise,
Have spent their instruments on lesser things,
And sung through all their compensated days
The worth of patrons wearing paper wings?
Their verses smelled of obligation's ink,
Their music creaked beneath the weight of hire,
And all their metaphors were made to drink
From wells that taste of nothing but desire.
But I have found a subject not for sale,
Whose virtue stands in need of no enlarge,
Whose least perfection makes my verse grow pale
And leaves me debtor who would be in charge:
    For flattery is easy — truth is hard,
    And thou hast made an honest of this bard.

CCXXV

The bee that sips from every blossom laid
Across the meadow's indiscriminate reach
Doth carry home a honey half decayed
By being gathered from beyond its teach.
But that same bee, directed to one bloom
Of sovereign sweetness none may replicate,
Brings back a thing so fragrant in its fume
As makes all other honey merely late.
So I, who wandered once through many loves
And gathered from each one a thinning store,
Have found at last the garden that behoves
A constant bee to ask of nothing more:
    For thou art that one blossom, sovereign, whole,
    Whose nectar fills and satisfies the soul.

CCXXVI

In silence there is much that speech cannot
Convey with all its instruments of sound,
For language is a garden, over-hot
With words that crowd where better seeds were found.
The pause between two notes of music holds
As much as either note on either side,
And in the space that no man's tongue unfolds
Doth all the weight of intimacy reside.
So when we sit and neither cares to speak,
And afternoon moves leisurely to dusk,
I find the eloquence that I most seek
Is housed within that comfortable husk:
    Thou need'st not say a word for me to know
    All that the noisiest language could not show.

CCXXVII

Let those who prize the lustre of a name
Build high their reputations in the crowd,
And court the warm periphery of fame
And wrap themselves in its uncertain shroud.
For fame is but the shadow of a thought
That multitudes have shared for half a breath,
A fragile currency too dearly bought
By those who've traded living close to death.
I have no use for that which crowds bestow
And withdraw again before the sun goes down,
For I have something crowds can never know,
A private and a more enduring crown:
    Thy love is what I'd wear against the tide,
    Worth all the fame that ever fame supplied.

CCXXVIII

The painter that would capture morning light
Must work with patience ere the moment flies,
For dawn will not be still for mortal sight
And gold turns grey before the beholder's eyes.
How much the greater task is mine therefore,
Who seek to fix not light but living grace,
To hold in words what words were not made for
And set thy spirit in a staying place.
My colours are but letters, black and thin,
My canvas but the fragile page of time,
Yet something of thee enters in between
The syllables of every earnest rhyme:
    What painters lose to light, I hope to keep —
    Thy truest likeness, not in paint, but deep.

CCXXIX

Why doth the rose decline within the week
That should by right be given many months?
And why doth beauty in the fairest cheek
Concede too soon to time's impatient stunts?
Nature, it seems, hath learned a cruel art
Of hoarding time from what deserves the most,
And giving freely to the dullest part
What finer things have never come to boast.
Yet in this very brevity there lies
A lesson that the permanent doth miss,
For what endures grows common to the eyes
While rarity lends sweetness to the kiss:
    Thou art more precious for the time thou'lt spend,
    And dearer for the knowing of thine end.

CCXXX

There is no art in loving what is fair
When fairness trumpets its own argument,
And beauty stands so naked in the air
That even blind affection finds its scent.
The harder and more honourable deed
Is loving through the mist of ordinary days,
When habit dulls the eye and tired need
Forgets to look with any wonder's gaze.
I mean to be that rarer kind of lover
Who sees thee not alone in festival
But in the plain light when no tricks discover
Thee other than thou art, in all in all:
    Not love that flares when beauty makes it easy,
    But love that holds when holding is not breezy.

CCXXXI

What is the compass worth upon the sea
If he who holds it does not know his port?
And what is all my faculty to me
If thou art not the haven of my thought?
The instrument is nothing without aim,
And aim is hollow without something true
To point it toward — I say this without shame,
All that I have is instruments for you.
My words are but the rigging of a ship
That finds its meaning only in the wind,
And all my art is but a nervous grip
Upon the wheel of all I've tried to find:
    Thou art the port, the wind, the chart, the sea —
    Without thee I am but the ship's debris.

CCXXXII

Some swear the night is enemy to thought
And take the early morning for their muse,
When all the quiet of the dark is brought
To heel by light and ordinary views.
But I have found my clearest thinking made
In that late hour when all the house is still,
And shadows constitute their own parade
And stars reveal their customary will.
For in the dark thy face is most exact,
Unbothered by the business of the day,
And memory performs its faithful act
Of bringing thee in full without delay:
    The day distracts me with its common things,
    But night restores thee on its quiet wings.

CCXXXIII

There was a time I thought the world too wide
And felt in its great spaces only small,
As one who tries to walk against the tide
And measures self against the ocean's wall.
But then thy hand came quietly to mine
And suddenly the world was not so broad,
For love doth work this ever-useful sign,
That vastness shrinks to fit a shared abode.
The sea is still the sea — I do not claim
That love hath somehow changed geography,
But what was once a wilderness became
A place of warmth and hospitality:
    The world was large until I found thy door,
    Then large seemed only small, and small seemed more.

CCXXXIV

Now let me close these verses as one shuts
A window on the evening, not in haste,
But with the care of one who never cuts
The end before the full moment is traced.
For I have loved thee in these numbered lines
As fully as my given art allows,
And if some stanza stumbles or repines,
Let effort be the witness of my vows.
The sonnets of the great lie long before
These humble efforts, casting lengths of shade,
Yet even in that shadow I adore
The chance to honour what thy presence made:
    Let greater poets speak of grander things —
    I'll spend my lesser gift on what love brings.
