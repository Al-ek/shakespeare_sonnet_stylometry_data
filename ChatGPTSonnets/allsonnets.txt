CLXXV

Why dost thou hide thy light in jealous shade,
As though the sun were debtor to the night?
What fear persuades thee beauty’s coin to fade,
And lock its treasure from the public sight?
The rose, though brief her sovereignty of hours,
Unfolds her wealth to every passing eye;
She hoards not fragrance in her guarded bowers,
Nor bids the wandering summer breeze deny.
If thou, self-prisoned in thy silent grace,
Dost keep thy glory sealed from common air,
Time’s subtle thief shall rifle every trace
And leave thee poorer than thou wert most fair.
Spend, then, thy riches ere they waste away,
For unused gold doth tarnish into clay.


CLXXVI

When first I saw thee in the morning’s pride,
The east grew pale, ashamed to match thy hue;
Day blushed to find his splendor magnified
Within the sovereign brightness lent by you.
Yet fleeting hours, like courtiers false and fleet,
Conspire to pluck the garland from thy brow;
They kneel at dawn, at dusk thy reign defeat,
And write their wrinkles where smooth lilies grow.
But virtue is a fortress not of sand,
Nor yields it tribute to the tyrant years;
Its marble stands when towers of beauty stand
No more, dissolved in penitential tears.
Let goodness be the mirror of thy face,
And time shall find no conquest in thy grace.


CLXXVII

What profit lies in solitary praise,
When echo only answers to thy song?
The sweetest lute, untouched for many days,
Grows harsh with silence, having slept too long.
So is thy heart, if barred from kind exchange,
A garden walled against the liberal spring;
Though fertile still, its blossoms suffer change,
Denied the commerce gentle seasons bring.
Unclose thy gates, and let affection sow
Those seeds that else in barren soil shall die;
For love that in another’s breast doth grow
Returns with harvest multiplied thereby.
Give forth thyself, and thou shalt richer be,
In losing self, thou gain’st eternity.


CLXXVIII

The glass that shows thee what thou art today
Doth flatter less than memory of youth;
It whispers softly how thy hairs turn gray,
And trades bright certainty for tempered truth.
Yet in those lines where careful years have writ
Their chronicles upon thy patient brow,
There dwells a wisdom none can counterfeit,
A deeper charm than summer doth allow.
Despise not age, that sober counsellor,
Whose furrowed hand bestows a steadier sight;
For painted dawn is but a promise poor
Compared with noon in full meridian light.
So let thy fading serve a fairer end:
In ripeness lives the harvest time doth send.


CLXXIX

If envy gnaw upon thy neighbor’s fame,
And bid thee measure worth by others’ gain,
Consider how the self-consuming flame
Devours the heart wherein it would remain.
The laurel thrives not by another’s fall,
Nor doth the eagle mount by clipping wings;
Each star maintains his station in the hall,
And from his proper sphere due luster brings.
Content thee with the orbit heaven gave,
And strive therein thy brightness to refine;
For he that would another’s glory have
Makes shipwreck of the compass of his own.
In thine own course let excellence be shown,
And thou shalt reign secure upon thy throne.


CLXXX

The tide of favor ebbs as swift it flows,
And fickle crowds their borrowed idols break;
Today they crown with garlands and with oaths,
Tomorrow’s breath the selfsame tongues forsake.
Trust not the trumpet’s loud but hollow cry,
Nor build thy dwelling on applause of men;
For windy praise doth into silence die,
And leaves thee poorer than thou wert ere then.
But if thy conscience stand a faithful guard,
And inward truth approve what thou hast done,
No outward tempest strikes thy peace so hard
As to eclipse the witness of the sun.
Seek thou that court where judgment is sincere,
And scorn the market of the vulgar cheer.


CLXXXI

When sorrow like a winter storm descends
And strips the branches of their summer cheer,
The heart, unmantled, to the tempest bends
And thinks no kindly April shall appear.
Yet roots that drink beneath the frozen ground
Prepare unseen the miracle of spring;
Though frost imprison all the fields around,
Within the core new-quickened pulses sing.
So grief, though harsh in its dominion brief,
May tutor hope in patience and in might;
For out of long endurance of our grief
Breaks forth a more resilient kind of light.
Then faint not thou beneath affliction’s rod,
For storms are but the husbandmen of God.


CLXXXII

The poet’s ink, though black upon the page,
Distills a brightness time cannot obscure;
In measured lines he wages subtle war
Against oblivion’s appetite obscure.
The marble cracks, the gilded statues fall,
Empires dissolve like dew at rising sun;
Yet in the slender compass of a scrawl
Breathes still the fame that mighty deeds have won.
So may my verse, though humble in its claim,
Preserve some shadow of thy living worth,
And snatch from mutability thy name,
Setting it firm upon enduring earth.
While tongues can speak and mortal eyes can see,
So long shall these poor lines remember thee.


CLXXXIII

Thou art a music wrought of varied strings,
Where mirth and melancholy intertwine;
Now joy in nimble, golden cadence springs,
Now sighing notes in minor fall and pine.
To choose but one were discord to the whole,
For harmony from contraries is born;
The shadow lends a contour to the soul,
And night prepares the splendor of the morn.
Despise not then the sadness in thy breast,
Nor chase it hence as though it were a foe;
It is the depth whereby thy joys are blessed,
The darker soil where richer roses grow.
Accept each mood as part of nature’s art,
And find full symphony within thy heart.


CLXXXIV

Ambition, winged with fire, would scale the height
Where fame upon a narrow summit stands;
Yet thin the air that feeds such perilous flight,
And weak the foothold of aspiring hands.
How many, dazzled by the distant gleam,
Have lost the safer path beneath their feet;
Pursuing still a half-imagined dream,
They stumble where success and ruin meet.
But he that climbs with measured, patient pace,
Esteeming more the journey than the prize,
Finds in each step a tempering of grace
And learns true greatness under humbler skies.
Aspire, yet let discretion be thy guide,
Lest mounting high, thou fall from wounded pride.


CLXXXV

The face thou show’st the world is but a veil,
A courteous mask for market and for street;
Behind it dwell desires that oft assail
The ordered calm that strangers think complete.
O let there be one chamber of thy breast
Wherein no counterfeit need find employ,
But truth, unarmed, may harbor and may rest
Secure from censure, rivalry, or noise.
For he that lives divided from his core
Doth waste his strength in endless self-defense;
While simple candor, fearing none before,
Stands like a tower of quiet confidence.
Be thou at one within thy secret heart,
And outward shows shall need no studied art.


CLXXXVI

The hours I spend in absence from thy sight
Move slow as shadows on a winter’s wall;
Each minute limps, bereft of its delight,
And heedless clocks in sullen cadence call.
Yet when thy presence blesses once again
The common air wherein we breathe and speak,
Time, erst so harsh, forgets his former pain
And grows prodigal of the joy we seek.
Thus art thou sovereign of my measured days,
Commanding swiftness or reluctant stay;
In thy regard lies hidden all the ways
By which my seasons flourish or decay.
O grant me but the tenure of thy glance,
And I shall thrive in love’s sweet governance.


CLXXXVII

Not steel nor stone the firmest bulwark prove,
When once corruption saps their inward might;
So vows unwatered by continual love
Grow brittle in the weathering of spite.
Renew thy promises with daily care,
As gardeners tend the vine with watchful eye;
For weeds of doubt spring rank in neglect there,
And choke the fruit that else would multiply.
Let kindness be the sunshine of thy speech,
And patience like a soft, unceasing rain;
So shall affection ripen out of reach
Of petty storms that breed from pride or pain.
In constant nurture let thy troth abide,
And love shall stand though lesser passions slide.


CLXXXVIII

The child thou wert still lingers in thine eyes,
Though manhood’s beard proclaim advancing years;
In sudden laughter innocence defies
The careful discipline of prudent fears.
Cherish that spark which time would fain suppress,
Lest custom’s yoke too heavily descend;
For wonder is the fountain of excess
From which imagination draws her friend.
Without it life grows mechanical and dry,
A ledger kept with uninspired hand;
But with it even common things may vie
With marvels scattered through a fabled land.
Preserve within thy breast that youthful flame,
And age shall find thee vigorous the same.


CLXXXIX

The night, though clad in sable, is not void,
But sown with stars that mock her seeming grief;
So in thy darkest hours are joys deployed,
Small lights that yield the spirit sweet relief.
Look up when earthly prospects fade from view,
And thou shalt see what daylight hid from sight;
For heaven reserves her clearer truths for few
Who dare to study constellations bright.
Despair is but a cloud that dims the scope,
Not an eclipse ordained to last for aye;
Behind its veil stands undiminished hope,
Prepared to chase the stubborn gloom away.
Trust that no shadow rules with endless sway,
For every night prepares a dawning day.


CXC

The breath of rumor, wandering and wild,
Makes monstrous shapes of trifles lightly born;
A careless word by malice reconciled
Returns in armor sharper than a thorn.
Guard well thy tongue, yet more thy listening ear,
That thou admit no falsehood to thy trust;
For slander thrives where men incline to fear
And feed suspicion with unwholesome gust.
Examine first, and judge with tempered mind,
Lest thou become the herald of a lie;
For once set free, no rein can rumor bind,
Nor call her back though truth protest and cry.
Let charity before thy verdict stand,
And mercy guide the sentence of thy hand.


CXCI

The sea of fortune alters with the moon,
Now lifting high, now leaving vessels bare;
He laughs at morning who by afternoon
May sit companioned only by despair.
Yet wisdom trims her sails to either tide,
Nor boasts in plenty nor in want repines;
She makes of adverse winds a lawful guide
And reads advantage in untoward signs.
So shalt thou steer amid uncertain chance,
If moderation hold the helm secure;
For extremes invite a perilous advance,
While steady courses longest shall endure.
In even temper let thy spirit be,
And thou shalt master fate’s inconstancy.


CXCII

The eye enamored of exterior show
May miss the jewel hid in modest dress;
For glittering surfaces too often throw
A gaudy veil on inward emptiness.
Seek thou the substance under painted guise,
And test with trial what first sight commends;
For truth withstands the scrutiny of eyes,
While falsehood at a searching glance soon bends.
So in thy friendships weigh not wealth nor face,
But probe the temper of the chosen heart;
There lies the measure of enduring grace,
Not in the ornaments of borrowed art.
Prize what is sound beneath the outward frame,
And thou shalt seldom entertain a shame.


CXCIII

If thou wouldst write thy story on the air,
In deeds of kindness traced from day to day,
Think not small acts unworthy of thy care,
For greatness oft in little things doth stay.
The cup of water offered in due hour
Outweighs the banquet spread for vain display;
A gentle word may wield a mightier power
Than trumpets summoning a proud array.
Thus shall thy memory in others live,
Not carved in stone, but cherished in the mind;
For hearts remember longest what we give
In quiet love, unheralded, yet kind.
Sow daily seeds of unpretending worth,
And reap a harvest in another’s earth.


CXCIV

At last when age shall close thy mortal eyes
And bid thee render up thy borrowed breath,
What treasure shall accompany thee rise
To plead thy cause against the bar of death?
Not titles, lands, nor flattering renown,
For these dissolve as frost before the sun;
But conscience clear shall be thy lasting crown,
And faithful love the record thou hast won.
Live then so well that when the summons comes,
Thou need’st not quake before the final sea;
Let virtue be the music of thy drums,
And hope the pilot of eternity.
So shalt thou pass, yet not to darkness driven,
For righteous lives make luminous their heaven.
