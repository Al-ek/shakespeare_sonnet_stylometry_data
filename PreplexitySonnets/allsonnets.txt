CCLV

From morning’s hush the gentle dawn is born,
And bathes the earth in blush of tender hue;
The rose awakens, shaking off the morn,
And greets the sun as lovers newly do.
Yet time, that thief, with stealthy fingers cold,
Doth pinch the bloom and steal its fragrant breath;
Thus beauty, frail as stories poets told,
Still walks with fleeting steps toward the death.
But who in art or song shall bind the day,
And chain the light that glimmers but an hour?
Only the heart, though bent and worn away,
Can keep within its walls that vanished flower.
Let me, through verse, preserve thy fleeting grace,
And snare the morning’s ghost within a place.
CCLVI

Thy voice once stirred the sleeping winds to song,
Now silence holds dominion in thy stead;
What once was right now bends to seem but wrong,
For echoes fade though words are never dead.
The trees remember whispers through their leaves,
And shadows mimic gestures once so kind;
Yet time, relentless, quietly deceives,
And leaves but footprints of thy vanished mind.
O fleeting hour! O sands that swiftly run!
How cruelly slips thy beauty from my hand;
Yet still I'll love thee till the setting sun
Bids light depart and night reclaim the land.
For though thou pass beyond what eyes may see,
Thy ghost shall live in verse and dream with me.
CCLVII

Look how the sea consumes the crumbling shore,
Yet builds anew with sands it once betrayed;
So hearts rebuild though broken oft before,
And rise from tears their own strong piers made.
The moon, half-hidden, glimmers through the storm,
And whispers comfort though its light is frail;
So tender love survives in altered form,
And sails through grief on hope’s uncertain sail.
Let loss, then, be the chisel to refine,
Not smash the marble where our trust is laid;
For in each scar some secret stars align,
And night is fairer for the dark it made.
So, though our sighs be carved from sorrow’s tone,
Each heart’s cathedral stands, though built of stone.
CCLVIII

O cruel glance that first unmade my peace,
And with thy fire did melt my guarded will;
Thou teachest joy and pain to never cease,
And mak’st sweet torment sweeter still.
I did not seek to serve a tyrant’s reign,
Yet crown thee Queen of every thought I keep;
My freedom lost is but a gentle chain,
For bonds of thine are softer than my sleep.
If prisoned I must be, then thus confined,
Let passion be the key and calm the cell;
For what is liberty to hearts entwined,
Or heaven’s gate compared to love’s deep spell?
So seal my fate, and write me in thine eyes,
For death is life if thou my soul baptize.
CCLIX

When first I traced thy likeness in a dream,
The stars bent close and whispered in delight;
They saw in thee what poets dare esteem—
A sun reborn within the cloak of night.
Thy glance commands what kings and gods obey,
And turns to gold each sorrow thou dost touch;
Even winter learns to bloom beneath thy sway,
And frost to flame beneath thy hands as such.
O soul of light, though days may wane and wear,
Thy form shall live in thought’s eternal spring;
Though dust be death’s unalterable share,
My verse shall make thy shade forever sing.
And when all tongues lie dumb, the world shall see,
How words once loved were monuments to thee.
CCLX

My pen, though frail, would carve thy tender name,
Upon the margin of the passing sky;
That all who read may bless the fleeting flame,
Which once on earth in living grace did lie.
No gold endures, nor empires built of stone,
But soft remembrance lasts beyond decay;
For memory makes the perished hour its own,
And steals from night the hues of vanished day.
Then let the future call me vain and weak,
For chasing ghosts that cannot once return;
Yet through this art thy silent lips shall speak,
And endless hearts thy vanished beauty learn.
So art shall triumph where dull time should reign,
And love itself be deathless in my vein.
CCLXI

The withered leaf returns not to the bough,
Yet whispers softly of the spring once fair;
So age recalls the bloom of beauty now,
And sighs to find but memory lingering there.
What treasure hath this world more pure than youth,
When heart sees truth unshadowed by disguise?
Yet wisdom, child of sorrow mixed with truth,
Turns tears to pearls and grief to clearer eyes.
Then let me age, so long as mind may keep,
The music made when love was freshly sung;
For while the world sinks softly into sleep,
My soul remains as ever fair and young.
And though time steal the mirror from my sight,
It leaves my heart still lit with inward light.
CCLXII

Why should I fear the tempest’s noisy breath,
When calm comes after wrath has had its way?
For peace is born of peril and of death,
And light shines clearer after brief dismay.
So let my griefs, like storms that cleanse the air,
Destroy the dust that clouds my inward view;
And when the thunder fades and skies are fair,
I’ll see the world made bright as if it’s new.
O patient heart, though tempests rage and toss,
Thou findest strength that trial only yields;
For through the rain blooms brighter every loss,
As sun returns to resurrect the fields.
Then count no storm a curse, but rather kind,
That gives the calm renewed to soul and mind.
CCLXIII

Behold the moon, though pale beneath the dawn,
Abides in faith until the night’s return;
So constant love, though oft by daylight wan,
Still keeps its flame and silently doth burn.
The fickle crowd may chase the sunlight’s show,
And scorn the lamp that glows when day has fled;
Yet I, more true, in quiet homage bow,
And find in night’s embrace my comfort spread.
Though distant stars be far beyond my touch,
Their beams descend to meet me where I lie;
So love, though parted, still reveals as much,
And binds the earth with promise from the sky.
What though my arms may never thee contain?
My soul ascends and meets thee dream again.
CCLXIV

Fain would I pluck one hour from passing years,
And hold it close, as miser guards his gold;
That time might freeze the laughter through my tears,
And stay the face I now too fond behold.
But time, unheeding, hurries on apace,
Nor hears the prayers of hearts that cling and cry;
He writes with dust the name of every grace,
And builds each joy to watch it crumble by.
Yet though he takes, he cannot all destroy,
For thought doth live where flesh must fall apart;
And memory breeds an everlasting joy
Within the boundaries of the faithful heart.
Then let him steal what hour he will from me,
My soul holds time in immortality.
CCLXV

Some praise in wealth their solace and their trust,
And think in gold to chide the grave away;
But what are gems except congealèd dust,
That shadows fade when set beneath decay?
The poorest heart, enriched by love’s bright store,
Hath greater state than monarch’s jewell’d brow;
For crowns fall down, but kindness standeth more,
And humble hearts outlast the pomp of vow.
Let princes boast their towers against the storm,
And merchants guard their coffers full of care;
The lover’s smile keeps even death in form,
And turns to spring the bitter breath of air.
Then count thy wealth not found in stone or steel,
But in the love thy faithful soul can feel.
CCLXVI

When silence reigns, I hear thy absent tone,
As if the air itself did thee repeat;
The wind becomes the whisper of thine own,
And night grows gentle pacing at thy feet.
No place so void that it contains thee not,
Thy shadow lingers where my eyes have been;
Each quiet corner keeps what time forgot,
And breathes thy ghost upon the sighing green.
If this be madness, teach me not the cure,
For sanity were colder than thy flame;
And better lost in dream that doth endure,
Than waking bound to life devoid of name.
So let me dwell where memory and hope meet,
And walk with thee in visions bittersweet.
CCLXVII

O gentle sleep, thou cruel saint of rest,
Why dost thou mock the weary with delay?
When most I crave, thou fliest from my breast,
And hides thy peace till sun divides the day.
Come, close my eyes, and bring that quiet deep,
Where thought dissolves and care is lull’d to none;
Then grant my soul an hour of tender sleep,
Where through my dreams I meet my absent one.
But shouldst thou bring no vision of her face,
Then stay, and keep me wakeful in my pain;
For better ache that guards her memory’s grace,
Than dull repose where she is not again.
O sleep, thou thief, restore what thou hast took,
Or leave me grief for comfort, not forsook.
CCLXVIII

When fate denies, the heart invents its door,
And builds through faith what earth to dust denies;
For souls have wings that mortal chains ignore,
And love hath sight though blind to outward eyes.
Thus when I lose, I learn to seek within,
Where boundless echoes answer every plea;
The world without may close, but not begin
To cage the thought that calls itself to thee.
So let men boast of kingdoms, swords, and law,
And count their triumphs vast as empire’s might;
Yet only hearts in truth and wonder draw
Their freedom from eternal inward light.
For love unseen still conquers all that dies,
And rules unthroned beneath unfinished skies.
CCLXIX

Once did I dream the stars were seeds of flame,
That heaven sowed across the boundless deep;
Each spark, a wish, a whisper, or a name,
That mortals plant within the fields of sleep.
If so, then thou art one such sacred seed,
Whose radiance grows wherever hope is sown;
And though the night my searching eyes impede,
Thy light still leads me till my path is known.
O wonder rare, that darkness bears the key,
And loss reveals what daylight could not show;
Thus even pain becomes a prophecy,
And tears the rain from which our gardens grow.
Then let the night disperse, or let it stay,
So long as thou art star to lead my way.
CCLXX

No voice can charm more deeply than the rain,
That falls with patience on the listening heart;
Its measured grief relieves the earth of pain,
And bids the seed of joy again to start.
So grief, though cold, doth nourish love concealed,
And sorrow’s soil breeds beauty’s secret root;
What grows from loss becomes the sweeter field,
That bends beneath remembrance’ ripened fruit.
Then let my tears not fall in vain to ground,
But water dreams that time shall yet restore;
The heart made rich through anguish most profound,
Shall bloom again, more steadfast than before.
For peace is earned when storm and sun agree,
And pain made holy turns to melody.
CCLXXI

How strange that eyes so bright could teach to blind,
And beauty’s gift become its own despair;
Yet thus the world’s design, by craft inclined,
To mix the thorn within what blooms most fair.
For love that never burned with fear to lose,
Would never learn how deep affection goes;
Nor could the heart its finest hues diffuse,
Till grief, hand-tender, brushed them as it rose.
So bless the wound that taught my soul to feel,
For through its ache I know that love was true;
And though it closed, it left a shining seal,
That lights my path with glimmer soft and new.
Then pain, once cursed, becomes my dearest friend,
That brought me truth, though not the hoped-for end.
CCLXXII

Let not the world persuade thee youth must fade,
For time bows only where the weak repine;
Age writes no ruin where the soul hath stayed,
But turns to depth what once was blazing shine.
Each wrinkle is a chapter love hath penned,
Each gray a crown from joy’s remembered fight;
And though the body's seasons know their end,
The inward sun maintains its steadfast light.
Then fear not age, nor count its slower pace,
For swifter hearts their own demise have won;
The sweetest fruit matures with time and grace,
And mellow hearts reflect the gentlest sun.
So live, and let the years their circle spin,
For beauty ripens most when held within.
CCLXXIII

What is our life but borrowed breath and flame,
A fleeting glow that trembles in the wind?
Yet in that spark eternity lays claim,
And whispers hope to who would not rescind.
For though we pass, our echoes still remain,
In song, in stone, in hearts that bear our will;
And love, once planted, blossoms not in vain,
But roots itself where time grows sound and still.
Then mourn no death, for nothing truly dies,
That once hath kindled goodness in the eyes;
The dusk will yield the dawn its brighter skies,
And hearts reborn shall all their loss despise.
So ends not life but shifts to fairer shore,
Where what we loved returns to us once more.
CCLXXIV

Now let my verse, though faint, outlast my years,
And bear my name when dust hath claimed my part;
For in these lines I leave my tender tears,
The map and measure of my faithful heart.
If none remember face or voice of mine,
Yet may they find within these words a soul;
For truth, though mortal tongues may not define,
Endures entire, unbroken, and whole.
Then carry, poem, what thy maker dreamed,
Across the dark where silent ages roll;
Let every heart that reads be faintly streamed,
With love’s bright fire that once made ashes whole.
And so, though gone, I shall not wholly fade,
For thou, my words, make time itself afraid.
