CLXXXII

The poet’s ink, though black upon the page,
Distills a brightness time cannot obscure;
In measured lines he wages subtle war
Against oblivion’s appetite obscure.
The marble cracks, the gilded statues fall,
Empires dissolve like dew at rising sun;
Yet in the slender compass of a scrawl
Breathes still the fame that mighty deeds have won.
So may my verse, though humble in its claim,
Preserve some shadow of thy living worth,
And snatch from mutability thy name,
Setting it firm upon enduring earth.
While tongues can speak and mortal eyes can see,
So long shall these poor lines remember thee.
